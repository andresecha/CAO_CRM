# What CIDOC-CRM, LRMoo, and CRMdig are, and why CAO_CRM combines them

When the Ariane consortium wanted to precisely describe a digitized literary corpus — think of an 18th-century novel preserved as a manuscript, published in several printed editions, and today also available as a scanned PDF in a digital library — it ran into a problem: no single existing vocabulary, taken on its own, allowed one to speak at once of the abstract idea of the work, of its concrete editions, and of the digital archive representing it today. That is why CAO_CRM (Corpus Author Ontology CRM) was not built from scratch, but by combining three already existing, internationally recognized "ontologies." An ontology, in this context, is simply a formal model: a set of categories ("classes") and relations between them, designed so that both people and computer programs can describe the same reality coherently and consistently. The three pieces CAO_CRM reuses are CIDOC-CRM, LRMoo, and CRMdig, each covering a distinct part of the problem.

## CIDOC-CRM: the general vocabulary of cultural heritage

CIDOC-CRM is a reference ontology for museums, archives, and libraries, adopted internationally for more than two decades and maintained by an international committee (the CRM-SIG). Its most characteristic trait — and the one the consortium's paper especially highlights — is that it does not focus so much on objects themselves as on the **events and processes** surrounding them: creation, transformation, transmission, conservation. The most general class of all, `E1 CRM Entity`, is defined as *"an abstract concept providing for three general properties: Identification by name or appellation... Classification by type... Attachment of free text"*: that is, the "umbrella" category from which all other classes in the model derive (persons, objects, places, periods, activities…), each identifiable, classifiable, and documentable through free-text notes.

This process-oriented focus is precisely what makes it useful for a literary corpus: it allows documenting who produced an object, when, and where, rather than merely describing its features. But, as the paper notes, *"s'il constitue un cadre particulièrement puissant pour décrire les objets patrimoniaux, il demeure relativement général"* ("while it constitutes a particularly powerful framework for describing heritage objects, it remains relatively general"). CIDOC-CRM does not distinguish, for instance, between the intellectual idea of a novel and each of its printed editions: for that, a more specific extension is needed.

## LRMoo: the bibliographic extension (work, expression, manifestation, item)

LRMoo is an extension of CIDOC-CRM built from IFLA's conceptual model (the International Federation of Library Associations), designed specifically for the bibliographic world. It introduces four central classes that let us separate levels everyday language tends to collapse under the single word "book." `F1 Work` is *"distinct intellectual ideas conveyed in artistic and intellectual creations"*: the idea of the work, independent of any concrete form it may take. `F2 Expression` are *"the intellectual or artistic realisations of Works... Differences in form imply different Expressions"*: each identifiable wording or textual version of that idea (a translation, a revised version). `F3 Manifestation` are *"products rendering one or more Expressions... defined by both the overall content and the form of its presentation"*: the concrete editorial form — a given printed edition, with its publisher, year, format — that materializes one or more expressions. And `F5 Item` designates the particular physical copy a reader can hold in their hands.

This work → expression → manifestation → item ladder is exactly what CIDOC-CRM lacked for handling literary corpora: it lets one say, for instance, that a single work has several editions without conflating them. But, as the paper cautions, *"si cette extension permet de prendre en charge la dimension bibliographique et éditoriale des textes, elle ne suffit toutefois pas à rendre compte des transformations liées à leur numérisation et à leur exploitation dans les environnements numériques contemporains"* ("while this extension covers the bibliographic and editorial dimension of texts, it is not sufficient to account for the transformations tied to their digitization and their use in contemporary digital environments"). LRMoo was designed for library catalogues, not for digital archives, scanned files, or digitization processes.

## CRMdig: the extension for digital objects and processes

CRMdig completes the picture by adding the vocabulary missing to describe the digital realm. Its central class, `D1 Digital Object`, is defined as *"identifiable immaterial items that can be represented as sets of bit sequences, such as data sets, e-texts, images, audio or video items, software, etc... A D1 Digital Object does not depend on a specific physical carrier"*: an identifiable digital file, independent of the physical medium on which it is stored. Alongside it, `D2 Digitization Process` covers the transition from a physical object to its digital version, typically through scanning or photography. CRMdig is, in the paper's words, the piece that *"permet de retracer la provenance des données et de documenter les différentes étapes de leur constitution"* (allows tracing data provenance and documenting the various stages of its creation), including the software used, formats, and successive transformations.

## Why all three had to be combined

None of the three pieces alone covers the full life cycle of a digitized literary corpus. The paper sums it up thus: *"le CIDOC CRM fournit le cadre général de représentation des objets culturels et de leurs trajectoires, LRMoo prend en charge la spécificité des œuvres et des objets bibliographiques, tandis que CRMdig permet d'intégrer leur dimension numérique ainsi que les processus techniques associés"* (CIDOC-CRM provides the general framework for representing cultural objects and their trajectories, LRMoo handles the specificity of works and bibliographic objects, while CRMdig integrates their digital dimension and the associated technical processes). CAO_CRM articulates all three precisely so as to be able to follow a single text — *Madame Bovary*, say — from the abstract idea of the novel, through its various printed editions, to the scanned PDF a researcher consults today in a digital library, without losing any of these levels along the way.
